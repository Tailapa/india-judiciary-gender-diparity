Causal Recipes of Exclusion

Dissecting Systemic Barriers in Indian Higher Judiciary

Author

Shashank Venkatesh

Published

12 January 2026

Abstract

This paper utilizes fsQCA to analyse the severe under-representation of women in India’s higher judiciary, a crisis threatening our institutional legitimacy, and global standing. We conceptualise this gender disparity using four causal factors: Structural, Institutional, Procedural and Socio-Cultural. By analysing all 25 high courts and the Supreme Court of India, this study aims to identify the specific necessary and sufficient causal configurations that sustain this disparity. At the end of this research, we aim to recommend targeted, evidence-based, and practical policy levers to facilitate representative and gender inclusive judicial institutions.

Introduction

India's ambition to be a developed nation by 2047 requires its judiciary to be representative, yet it's higher judicial institutions are under-representative of women, highlighted by past controversial verdicts which necessitate inclusive benches. Prior research failed to holistically capture the complex combination of systemic barriers that sustain Gender Disparity (D). This study’s objective is to utilise fuzzy-set Qualitative Comparative Analysis (fsQCA) to overcome D by identifying causal configurations among Structural (S), Institutional (I), Procedural (P), and Socio-Cultural (C) factors to propose evidence-based policy reforms.

Motivation and Objective

The Crisis

  • Only 9% female representation in higher judiciary.
  • Comparison: USA (44%), UK (25%), Canada (44%). [cite: 24]

Impact

Such compromised institutions may not have the moral authority to deliver effective justice and may lead to controversial verdicts as seen in Mathura Case (1978) and Re: Matter of Great Public Importance…(2019).

Research Question

How do complex causal factors like Structural (S), Institutional (I), Procedural (P), and Socio-Cultural (C) barriers interplay to sustain D in Indian higher judiciary?

NOTE: There has never been a woman Chief Justice of India, despite nearly 80 years of independence

Concepts & Measurement

Defining Gender Disparity (D) in the Higher Judiciary (H)

D in H of India is defined as a sustained, severe, and systemic shortfall in the representation of women judges in the Supreme Court (SC) and High Courts (HC). This lack of gender diversity visibly impacts the institutional legitimacy and global standing of the courts.

  • Conceptual Framework: D (Level 1 or L1) is captured holistically employing Gary Goertz’s conceptualization (Social Science Concepts and Measurement, 2020).
  • Macro-Dimensions: The framework consists of four constituent conditions serving as Level 2 (L2) causal factors.
  • Micro-Dimensions: L2 dimensions are measured using 10 specific indicators (Level 3 or L3) as detailed in Table 1 and visualized in Figure 1.

Table 1: Conceptualization and Measurement of Gender Disparity (D) using Gary Goertz’s framework

Macro Dimensions (L2) Micro Dimensions (L3 Indicators)
Structural Factors (S)

1. Network Support Deficit Index (NSDI): Percentage of women candidates appointed to HCs/SC without inherited legal family background, compared to male counterparts.

2. Vertical Representation Gap (VRG): The ratio of women's representation in HCs to their representation in the SC.
Institutional Factors (I)

1. Infrastructure Compliance Index (ICI): Percentage of HCs with functional gender-sensitive amenities (e.g., creches).

2. Policy Gap Score (PGS): Whether Policy exist mandating reservation.

3. Accessibility Index (AI): Measures the gendered financial barriers to entering the legal network.
Procedural Factors (P)

1. Opacity of Collegium Appointments (OCA): Percentage of Collegium resolutions that lack justifications for selection/rejection of women.

2. Supersession Ratio Disparity (SRD): Frequency with which eligible women HC judges are superseded by junior male judges for elevation.

3. Ratio of Discretionary Delay (RDD): Ratio of the average time taken by Executive to notify appointments of women vs. male judges.
Socio-Cultural Factors (C)

1. Time Use Disparity Index (TUDI): Differential time in hours spent by women vs. men judges on unpaid domestic care work.

2. Gendered Media Scrutiny Index (GMSI): Percentage of media coverage focusing on women judges’ personal lives over professional merit, compared to male judges.

Research Method: 4-Step fsQCA

We propose to utilize fuzzy-set Qualitative Comparative Analysis (fsQCA) to determine the Necessary and Sufficient Conditions (N/S) for sustaining Gender Disparity (D) in the Higher Judiciary (H). This method is optimal for analyzing small to medium samples (N=26), facilitating the identification of "causal recipes"—specific configurations of conditions that consistently lead to the outcome.

Causal Conditions
{S, I, P, C}
Primary Outcome
D

Execution Pipeline

1 Step 1: Data Collection and Case Selection

  • Case Selection:

    The sample comprises all 25 HCs and the SC of India (N=26) from 1993 onwards. Each institution serves as a unique case for the fsQCA analysis.

  • Data Sources:
    • Official records (Dept. of Justice, SC/HC websites)
    • Periodic reports (India Justice Report)
    • Interviews (Judges and Advocates)
    • Quantitative analysis of judicial tenure, and Collegium resolutions (through RTI)
    • Qualitative analysis (social media/print media reports for GMSI, policy analysis for PGS).

2 Step 2: Calibration and Fuzzy-Set Score Assignment

  • Converted analyzed/measured L3 indicators into fuzzy-set membership scores ranging from 0 to 1.
  • Calibration of indicators (L3, macro-dimensions S, I, P, C, and outcome D) produces a unified membership score for each case.
  • Aggregation of L3 indicators into L2 dimensions is performed using additive logic within the fsQCA framework.
0 (Full Non-Member): RDD <= 1.0
0.5 (Crossover): RDD = 1.5
1 (Full Member): RDD >= 2.5

3 Step 3: Identifying N/S Conditions

Necessary (N) Test: Condition must always be present when D occurs. Hence, the fuzzy-set score for all L2 dimensions across each case need to be consistent (must be > 0.9). If not, the condition is not necessary.

Sufficient (S) Test: To identify which configurations of L2 dimensions (e.g., S*P + I*C) are sufficient for D to occur. Hence, the combination must cover at least 80% cases and the corresponding L2 dimensions need to maintain consistent fuzzy-set score > 0.8.

4 Step 4: Interpretation and Policy Recommendations

The execution of this research exercise would enable us to identify the combination of Causal Conditions that are N/S for D to occur across all cases. It would also facilitate comparative analysis of each case, enabling us to identify the barriers that are specific to a particular institutional context.

This would provide us with evidence-based and context-specific practical levers for policy intervention, fulfilling our objective of presenting targeted solutions rather than generic mandates.

Conclusion

This design, driven by fsQCA analysis will successfully isolate the parsimonious causal pathway sustaining D in Indian H. By identifying the N/S conditions from the complex causal configurations, the study moves beyond generic solutions. Once executed, the findings will provide the relevant stakeholders with practical, evidence-based policy interventions tailored to specific institutional contexts, achieving the urgent need for a truly inclusive, and credible higher judiciary.
